Decision Threshold
Why threshold = 0.30 (not 0.50)?
In churn prevention, missing a churner (false negative) costs roughly 5× more than incorrectly flagging a loyal customer (false positive). Using a lower threshold means we catch more true churners at the cost of some false alarms — the right trade-off for a retention team with capacity to act. The threshold was optimized by maximizing F1 over a search grid from 0.10 to 0.90.

FAQ
Why is the threshold 0.30 and not 0.50?
A 0.50 threshold treats false positives and false negatives equally. In churn prevention, missing a churner costs ~5× more than flagging a loyal customer incorrectly. We tune the threshold to reflect this real-world business cost, optimizing F1 over a grid search.
What is SMOTETomek and why does it matter?
The Telco dataset has ~26% churn — a class imbalance. SMOTETomek combines synthetic oversampling of the minority class (SMOTE) with Tomek links removal to clean noisy borderline samples. This produces better class separation than oversampling alone.
Can I use this for my own customer data?
Yes — for screening and decision support. Column names in your CSV must match the required format (case-insensitive). Geography should be encoded as France=0, Germany=1, Spain=2. For production use on a different market, retraining on your own data is recommended.
What does the batch CSV output look like?
Your original columns are returned unchanged, with two new columns appended: churn_probability (0.0–1.0 float) and prediction (0 = will stay, 1 = predicted to churn at the 0.30 threshold).
